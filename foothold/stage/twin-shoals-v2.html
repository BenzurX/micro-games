1 — Shared coastline budget

The shore always grows from the top-right and bottom-left corners - same two corners as the shipped level, always opposite the two home bases, so a shoreline can never swallow a starting zone. What changes is how the two wedges are sized: instead of rolling each corner's depth independently, they now split a single combined tile budget, so one greedy roll can never balloon past what the other corner leaves room for - which is exactly what caused the reported 6+15 tile bug.

Design notes

Corners are fixed, not pooled: top-right and bottom-left, every game, same as the shipped level - the corner-pool idea (drawing 2 of 4 at random) is dropped since it could occasionally pair two corners adjacent to the same home, or even land a wedge on a home's own corner. Locking to TR/BL keeps both starting zones guaranteed dry no matter what the budget split rolls. Tab 2 covers the "shore doesn't have to fit a clean diagonal" part of your note - the shape filling each of these two fixed regions can still vary.

Shared budget, not independent rolls: roll one combined tile-count budget for the whole game (e.g. 8-14 tiles total, ~15-26% of the 54-tile board), then split it unevenly between the two chosen corners (30-70% to one side) so asymmetry is preserved - one corner can still be noticeably bigger, just never both-big at once. Each corner's tile budget converts to the nearest ring depth (2 -> 3 tiles, 3 -> 6, 4 -> 10, 5 -> 15, same triangular table as today), clamped to depth 2-5 like before.

Net effect: the coastline is still different and asymmetric every game - the whole point of the original sketch - but the map can no longer get choked by an unlucky double-high roll, and the shoreline's identity (which side of the board it's on) now varies game to game instead of always reading as "the two corners opposite home."

2 — Organic blob coastline

Your notebook sketch isn't a clean 45° wedge growing out of a corner point, and it's not a smooth edge run either - it's a genuinely irregular blotch, thicker in some spots, thinner or absent in others, with no straight boundary anywhere. The previous version of this tab still generated shapes from a depth-per-row/column profile, which is mathematically a smoothed diagonal no matter how much jitter gets added - that's why it kept reading as "still a wedge" even after the bias tweaks. This version drops profiles entirely and grows each shoreline as a random blob: one tile seeds near the corner, then it spreads outward one random neighboring tile at a time until it hits its budget. There's no formula for the boundary at all, so it can't come out diagonal or symmetric - every reroll is a genuinely different, lumpy shape. One blob is always guaranteed in the top-right region and one in the bottom-left region (same corners the shipped level uses), but neither is forced to touch the exact corner tile or hug an edge.

Design notes

Random blob growth, not a formula: pick one random seed tile inside a loose box near the target corner (not the corner tile itself - it can land a few tiles off). Then repeat: list every board tile adjacent to the current blob that isn't already in it and isn't a home tile, pick one at random, add it. Keep going until the region's tile budget is used up. Because the next tile is always a random pick from whatever's touching the current shape, the result is a lumpy blot with no consistent edge or angle - there is no depth/profile/wedge math involved anywhere, so it structurally cannot come out as a diagonal line or an even curve.

Fringe = the blob's actual boundary: any blob tile that touches at least one non-blob tile on the board (dry land, or a home) becomes the tidal fringe; every other blob tile is permanent open ocean. Since the blob's shape is irregular, the fringe ring is irregular too - it traces whatever ragged boundary the growth happened to produce, instead of a clean outer ring like the old wedge model.

Guaranteed regions, shared budget: every game grows exactly one blob near the top-right region and one near the bottom-left region (never zero, never both piled onto one side), and the two blobs still split a single combined tile budget (10-17 tiles, 30-70% split) so neither can balloon past what the other leaves room for - same fix as tab 1, just applied to a shape that isn't a wedge anymore.

Why this actually solves it: the earlier "biased edge run" was still a depth value per row or column, smoothed - that's a diagonal by construction, jitter or not. Swapping to tile-by-tile random growth removes the underlying math that kept producing wedges, rather than trying to disguise it with more randomness on top.

3 — Tide indicator + water shimmer ideas

Today the only tide "read" is the tile highlight (cyan pulsing outline on the fringe during rising/falling) plus a plain HUD text line. Four other ways to telegraph the clock, plus a few water-shimmer treatments for the permanent ocean tiles themselves (currently a flat fill).

A - Tide Ring
Lowheld

Circular 5-segment clock

A small ring icon in the HUD corner (near the round timeline). The filled arc position IS the phase - rising fills clockwise, high tide is a full ring, falling drains back down. Glance-readable without reading text.

B - Water Gauge
Low tide - 2 turns until it rises

Literal water-level meter

A small vertical tank next to the HUD, water line rising/draining with a crawling wave-crest texture on top. Doubles as a countdown ("2 turns until it rises") without extra text parsing - the fill height already says how close.

Pairs with a single small text line for players who want the exact count.

Design notes

A - Tide Ring: most compact, fits next to the existing round-timeline HUD element without competing for space. Weakest at conveying "how many turns until it moves" at a glance - needs the label text to carry that part.

B - Water Gauge: best at answering "how long do I have" since the fill height and the turn-count text reinforce each other. Takes the most HUD real estate of the four.

C - Phase Strip: closest to how the round timeline already reads (a strip of discrete steps), so it would feel like a natural sibling widget rather than a new visual language. Good candidate if HUD consistency matters more than compactness.

D - Ambient board-only: zero new HUD chrome, cheapest to build, and keeps the player's eye on the board instead of a corner widget - but is the easiest to miss on a first playthrough, and offers no explicit turn-countdown at all without a text line.

Decided: going with D (Ambient board-only) - no new HUD widget, the fringe tile highlight and HUD text line carry the tide read. Ocean-tile shimmer is also decided - Layered Swell (two crossing bands, from the round-2 iterations in stage/ocean-shimmer.html) - it's already wired into the tab 1 and tab 2 demo boards above (shim-6 class).
