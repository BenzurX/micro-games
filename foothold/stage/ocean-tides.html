Ocean level — tide ideas

Future-idea stage only (Todo.md "Future Ideas" -> Ocean level, not v1 scope) - a design sketch for what a rising/falling tide terrain gimmick could look like. Distinct from the shipped river: the river is a static wall with two fixed bridge crossings, and the tide is now the same kind of feature - a small, fixed shoreline zone placed once on the map. Only the 1-2 tiles right at that zone's edge move in or out each cycle and clear ownership when they submerge; everything outside the zone sits as normal dry ground the whole time.

Rough visual sketch, no real game engine or persistence - canned demo cycles only. Four variants below show different shapes and placements for the fixed shoreline zone; each has its own small illustrative board (not full rules) and a "Run tide cycle" button that plays one full rise-and-recede pass. Home tiles are exempt from flooding in all four (a base drowning would be a strange way to lose - worth a deliberate decision, not an accident of this sketch).

Tide variant

1 — Rolling Shoreline

A fixed shoreline zone sits in the board's bottom-left corner (4 tiles wide, 3 deep). Everywhere else on the board is dry, untouched ground - only that corner ever gets wet.

Low tide - dry

Only the bottom-left corner zone ever changes. Inside it, the waterline rolls in and out by one row per cycle; every tile outside the zone stays dry the whole time. Blue/red tiles caught under the moving row lose their owner the instant they submerge.

You Enemy Unclaimed, dry Wave crossing (about to change) Submerged
Design notes

Mechanic: a shoreline zone is placed once, at map-build time, in the board's bottom-left corner (4 cols x 3 rows). Nothing outside that rectangle is ever touched by the tide - no flood amount is even computed for those tiles. Inside the zone, the row closest to the board edge is the "sea" and stays wettest, the innermost row is the "high ground" and stays driest; only the middle row actually flips between dry and submerged each cycle, so the visible change is always just that one row.

Tactical implication: the shoreline becomes a specific, learnable piece of the map - players can see it, plan around it, and fight over the one row that actually moves, instead of the tide being an ambient board-wide tax nobody can route around. The tradeoff: with the zone fixed in one corner, it will naturally matter more to whichever home sits closer to it, so the corner should be picked (or mirrored) deliberately per board rather than left to whichever half the level generator favors.

Cheapest of the four to build and by far the easiest for a new player to read at a glance - a good baseline/default even if a fancier variant ships as the "real" ocean level.

2 — Cove Sweep

A small L-shaped wedge of 6 tiles is tucked into the top-right corner, like a little cove cut into the map. Nothing outside that wedge ever floods.

Low tide - dry

Only the top-right wedge ever changes. The corner tile floods first, the wedge's outer edge (1-2 tiles) floods and drains each cycle, and everything else on the board stays dry.

You Enemy Unclaimed, dry Wave crossing (about to change) Submerged
Design notes

Mechanic: six tiles form a small L/wedge cluster fixed in the top-right corner (the corner tile itself plus two short arms running along the top and right edges). Diagonal distance from the corner tile ranks the wedge into three depth layers; only the outermost layer (1-2 tiles) is ever mid-transition at a given moment, so the wedge reads as a compact cove, not an expanding board-wide wavefront. Every tile outside the wedge returns zero flood, always.

Tactical implication: because the wedge is small and diagonally shaped rather than a flat band, it creates one distinctly-shaped contested pocket that plays differently from variant 1's straight edge - good for making the two variants feel geographically distinct if both ever show up on different maps. Tradeoff: a 6-tile wedge tucked in one corner is a much smaller stake than a full edge zone, so it will read as a minor local skirmish rather than a defining board feature unless the corner is placed somewhere both players actually care about.

Reads as more organic/dynamic than the rolling shoreline's straight edge, while staying just as bounded and learnable once a player has seen the wedge once.

3 — Tide Pools

Three tiny fixed pools, each capped at a 3x3 footprint, flood outward from their own centre tile and recede on their own clock. Nothing outside those three small footprints ever gets wet.

Low tide - dry

Watch the three pools - they don't rise and fall in sync, so at any moment one might be flooding while another is already dry again. Each pool's rim only ever moves 1-2 tiles per cycle; the centre tile and everything beyond the 3x3 footprint never change.

You Enemy Unclaimed, dry Wave crossing (about to change) Submerged
Design notes

Mechanic: three fixed epicenters are placed once, scattered around the board (away from both homes). Each pool's flood radius is capped so its footprint never exceeds a 3x3 block of tiles - the centre floods deepest and stays wet longest, the rim (1-2 tiles) is the only part that actually flips between dry and submerged each cycle, and anything past that radius reads zero flood regardless of tide level. Each pool also runs on its own offset timer, so the three are never in the same phase at once.

Tactical implication: this was already the closest of the four to what a bounded shoreline should feel like, just needed a tighter footprint - now each pool reads as a specific, memorizable 3x3 spot worth fighting over (grab the rim right before it drowns, or rush back the moment it dries) instead of a diffuse zone with a fuzzy edge. Tradeoff: three independent small clocks are more to track than one shared waterline, so each pool still needs its own clear tell (distinct ripple/sound) so players aren't stuck staring at the whole board to catch the moment.

Most complex of the four to read at a glance since the three pools aren't synchronized, but now that each is small and capped it's a manageable kind of complex rather than an overwhelming one.

4 — Pincer Tide

Two small fixed shoreline strips, one tucked by each player's home flank (not the board's centre), rise and fall a single tile at a time. Everywhere else - including the whole middle of the board - is never touched.

Low tide - dry

Two 1-tile-wide strips, one by each home's flank, move in and out together by a single tile each cycle. The rest of the board, including the whole middle strip, stays dry the whole time.

You Enemy Unclaimed, dry Wave crossing (about to change) Submerged
Design notes

Mechanic: instead of both board edges squeezing the entire vertical span, two 1-tile-wide, 2-tile-deep strips are fixed near each player's home flank (the side column next to their home, not the board's centre). Each strip's outer tile (closest to the board edge) is the one that actually flips between dry and submerged; the inner tile and everything beyond the strip never changes. Both strips move together, in sync, so the pressure lands on both players at the same moment.

Tactical implication: each player now defends one small, specific tile next to their own home instead of watching a board-wide squeeze eat the whole middle - the tide becomes a home-flank skirmish both sides can plan around, not an event that erases the board's playable area. It stays symmetric by construction (both strips move on the same clock), so neither player is structurally favored. Tradeoff: at this small a scale the tide barely affects overall board control - it's a minor, local pressure point near each home rather than a map-shaping "crunch" beat, so it may need pairing with something else if the design still wants a bigger structural squeeze somewhere.

5 — Twin Shoals

Built from your notebook sketch: a permanent open-ocean wedge sits in the two corners opposite each home (top-right and bottom-left), and exactly one ring of shoreline tiles hugging that ocean tides in and out. Each corner's size is randomized independently every game - like the river's crossing points - so one corner is often much bigger than the other, and which side is bigger changes game to game.

Turn 4 of 5 - Low tide (held)

Tide runs on a fixed 5-turn clock: floods in over 1 turn, holds high for 1 turn, drains out over 1 turn, then holds low for 2 turns before repeating. Click "Next Turn" to step through it. "Reroll coastline" regenerates both corners' size and shape from scratch, the way a fresh game would.

Design notes

Asymmetric, randomized coastline: each corner's wedge is grown diagonally from its corner tile by a random depth (2-5 diagonal rings), chosen independently per corner per game - same idea as the river's crossing points never landing in the same place twice. Some games the top-right corner is a small 3-tile nub while the bottom-left sprawls into a 15-tile bay; other games it's reversed, or both are modest. Exactly one diagonal ring bordering whatever wedge got rolled is the tidal fringe - the only tiles that ever change state, regardless of how big the wedge is that game. Resources use the board's normal spread for both players; whichever ones happen to land inside a given game's wedge or fringe are simply the ones at risk that game, not a hand-picked "always drowned" tile.

Turn timing: the tide runs on a fixed 5-turn clock rather than a continuous animation - floods in over 1 turn, holds high for 1 turn, drains out over 1 turn, holds low for 2 turns, repeat. That gives players a full 2-turn dry window to plan and execute a claim near the shoreline before it starts moving again, with the in/out turns themselves reading as a clear single-turn "this is happening now" event rather than a background gradient.

Tactical implication: because the wedges sit in the corners away from both homes, the shoreline isn't a home-flank skirmish (that's variant 4's job) - it's contested, lower-priority edge territory that rewards whoever's willing to path out there and time a claim around the flip. Randomized asymmetry means players can't memorize "the ocean level always plays like this" - each match's coastline has to be scouted and read fresh.
