Fire level — hazard mechanic ideas

Future-idea stage only (Todo.md "Future Ideas", not v1 scope) — four candidate hazard mechanics for a lava/hell-themed level, each distinct from the shipped v1 river terrain. All hazard tiles punish whoever holds them; the tactical question in every idea is how much risk a player accepts to sit near fire.

Rough visual sketch only, no game engine or persistence. Each demo button plays a canned animation once — click it, then "Reset" to replay.

1 — Volcano meteors

You Enemy Burning Scorched (neutral, reclaim it) Volcano (impassable, fixed)

Design notes

The idea: the volcano is a fixed on-board feature now - a solid 2x2 cluster of impassable tiles sitting in the middle of the map, not off-screen scenery. Neither player can claim or act on those tiles; it's just a permanent landmark the match is played around. Instead of erupting on a turn-based cadence, it runs on its own real-time clock - the bar above fills continuously in the background and, the moment it completes, the volcano fires 3-4 meteors weighted toward already-claimed or contested tiles, then the bar resets and starts filling again.

Tactical implication: because that clock keeps running whether or not it's your turn, you can't "wait out" an eruption the way you can plan around a turn-counted hazard - it can fire mid-decision on your own move, or while you're just sitting idle waiting on the opponent. That's a genuinely different pressure from the other three lava ideas (all turn-paced) and from the rest of Foothold's level concepts generally, since the core game is turn-based throughout - this is the one place real time intrudes on it.

Feel: still bursty and dramatic - a few seconds of chaos, then calm - but the anxiety is ambient now instead of scheduled. Watching that bar creep toward full while you're trying to plan a move (yours or not) is the whole point: the board itself should feel like it's on a fuse.

Open question: this is a bigger architectural lift than the other three ideas, worth flagging honestly - Foothold's loop is otherwise fully turn-based, so a live clock needs a real answer for how it behaves around anything that pauses play (does it keep ticking behind a pause menu or settings modal? during the AI's "thinking" delay? on a game-over screen?). "Always ticking except on an explicit pause" is probably the simplest safe default, but that needs a real pause state to hook into, which doesn't exist elsewhere in the game yet.

2 — Lava snake

Snake head Snake body Telegraphed (next 1-2 moves) Scorched in its wake

Design notes

The idea: a lava-elemental worm crawls a winding path across the board over several turns, classic Snake-style movement. Any tile it currently occupies instantly loses ownership — scorched to neutral the moment the worm passes over it, whether it was yours or the enemy's.

Tactical implication: unlike the meteors (bursty and semi-random) this is a slow, readable threat. Because the next 1-2 tiles ahead of the head are shown as a dim telegraphed preview, a sharp player can pull value out of a tile before the worm arrives, or deliberately avoid building right in its path — it rewards reading ahead rather than reacting.

Feel: creates a moving "no-go" corridor that reshapes the board over time, more like a weather system than an attack. Could be tuned to move every N rounds so it's a background rhythm rather than the main event of the level.

Open question: should the worm's path be fully fixed per game (procedurally generated once, same fairness guarantee as node placement) or adapt toward whichever player is ahead — the latter is spicier but risks feeling unfair on mobile.

3 — Ember node

You Ember node, producing (warm pulse) Ember node, taxing (sharp flash)

Design notes

The idea: a hazard-adjacent resource node that produces noticeably more income than a normal node while you hold it, but periodically "flares" and burns a chunk of your stockpile as a tax for sitting on it. Genuine high-risk/high-reward, distinct from the other three ideas which are all about losing the tile itself — here the tile stays yours, the pain is a recurring stockpile hit.

Tactical implication: claiming it is a bet that the extra income outpaces the periodic tax over the rounds you expect to hold it — probably correct early (thin stockpile, high value-per-turn) and risky late (a big flare can wipe out a war chest right before a push). Gives late-game players a reason to actively decide whether to keep or abandon it rather than passively upgrading everything.

Readability is the whole design problem here: a player must be able to tell "is this about to tax me" at a glance, so the two states use deliberately different language — a slow warm amber pulse while producing safely, versus a sharp orange-red flash burst when it fires. A floating "-N gold" popup on flare makes the cost legible immediately rather than players noticing it only in the resource counter.

4 — Spreading wildfire

Ignition point / burned Firebreak (player-held, blocks spread) Enemy (consumed if reached)

Design notes

The idea: fire ignites at one fixed point (e.g. an edge tile prone to catching) and, if left unchecked, spreads tile-to-tile along adjacency once per round, consuming whoever owns each newly-ignited tile. Unlike the snake (which moves along a path) or the meteors (which strike at random), this is a growing front that fills outward from a single source — closer to a wildfire than a projectile.

Tactical implication: a player can actively contain it by holding a tile adjacent to the fire front as a firebreak — as long as they keep that tile claimed, the fire cannot cross it, and visibly deflects around to spread through other open tiles instead. This turns "defend this specific tile" into a real decision with a clear payoff, which the settled design pillars call out as something the game currently lacks (defense was cut in favor of Upgrade as stone's sink) — this could be the level-specific way to reintroduce it without touching the base ruleset.

Feel: the slowest-building of the four ideas — dread rather than a jump-scare. Good for a level that wants players to make an active choice every round ("do I spend an action to hold the firebreak, or let this side of the board burn and expand elsewhere") rather than just reacting to a random event.

Open question: does losing the firebreak tile (e.g. to enemy siege) let the fire resume spreading from where it stopped, or does the fire simply treat a freshly-scorched tile the same as any other burnable tile going forward? The latter is simpler to implement.
