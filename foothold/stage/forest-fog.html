Forest level — fog-of-war ideas

Future-idea stage only (Todo.md "Future Ideas", not v1 scope) — a design sketch for what a fog-of-war terrain gimmick could look like, no game logic wired in. Concept: enemy tiles/nodes outside your vision radius are hidden until one of your tiles is adjacent to them. Pick a fog treatment (A/B/C), then click any lit tile adjacent to your territory to "claim" it and watch fog peel back around your new vision.

This is a rough visual sketch, not the real board (no river/bridges modeled here). Real implementation would also need AI vision logic, not just new art — see the Future Ideas note in Todo.md.

Design notes

A — Flat dark: cheapest, reads instantly as "unknown." Now a single connected sheet (not per-tile) with a faint drift, so it no longer looks like a grid of separately-painted squares.

B — Soft vignette: darker toward the board edges, lighter toward the middle, with the same slow drift — feels like the fog is thinner near where you're standing.

C — Foliage canopy: a dark mossy base under two slow-drifting grey-white mist layers, now painted once across the whole board so the canopy blobs and mist banks are continuous from tile to tile instead of each tile carrying its own isolated pattern. Priciest of the three to build well for real (would want a proper Aseprite/painted fog texture and sprite animation instead of CSS gradients).

Reveal holes are soft-edged (blurred), not hard tile cutouts, so newly-scouted ground fades in rather than snapping — reads more like sight than a spreadsheet toggle.

Not shown: fog re-covering a tile if you lose vision of it (e.g. the claiming tile is recaptured) — worth deciding before implementation, since "once seen always seen" vs. "fog returns" are very different tactical feels.

You Unclaimed, scouted Fogged Bramble (neutral, blocked)
Design notes

What it is: a neutral, unaligned third force that lives deep in unscouted fog. If a chunk of the map goes too many rounds without being scouted, the Wildwalker raids: it seizes 1-2 random unclaimed (or lightly held) tiles at the fogged edge and covers them in thorny brambles.

Ownership: brambled tiles belong to neither player until someone pays an extra clearing cost to reclaim them — they're neutral but blocked, not free real estate.

Design point: punishes turtling and ignoring your own fog. It adds unpredictable third-party pressure so the map isn't purely a two-player tug of war around one AI opponent.

Not shown: the actual "rounds unscouted" counter and raid-frequency tuning — this demo just fires the raid on click. Real implementation needs a per-tile timer and a rule for how far into fog a raid can reach.

Stage 0 - base cost Stage 1 - +cost Stage 2 - ++cost Stage 3 - +++cost
Design notes

What it is: unclaimed tiles that stay unscouted (still fogged) for too many rounds slowly grow denser vines — visually darkening and thickening the longer they're ignored.

Cost: each overgrowth stage adds a small gold-cost surcharge to claiming that tile, shown as a rising badge in the corner. Scouting a tile resets its overgrowth.

Design point: rewards active scouting pace over turtling — the map itself pushes back against players who sit still, without needing a third-party actor like the Wildwalker.

Tiles in this demo advance in lockstep with the shared round counter but start at staggered stages, so a real board would show a mix of fresh and heavily overgrown tiles at once depending on when each was last seen.

Future-idea stage — a wandering owl perches on one random fogged tile. Whoever claims that specific tile gets a bonus vision radius while they hold it, then the owl moves on and you decide whether to chase it again.

Claim the tile the owl is perched on for a bonus vision radius, bigger than a normal claim's sightline, while you hold it. After a few rounds it moves on to a new fogged tile elsewhere on the board — you'll have to decide whether to chase it again or hold your ground.

Your claimed tile Claimed tile with owl (bonus vision) Fogged tile 🦉 Owl's current perch
Design notes

What it is: a wandering owl perches on one random fogged tile at a time. Whichever player claims that specific tile gets a larger-than-normal vision radius while they hold it. After some number of rounds, the owl relocates to a new random fogged tile elsewhere on the board.

Why it's clearer than the old Stealth Strike idea: vision itself is the prize, not a discount calculation layered on top of a symmetric-fog rule. There's one moving landmark to track and one decision to make - is it worth going after - instead of hidden math about who can see whom.

Tactical implication: you can turtle and let the owl wander off into ground you don't care about, banking on safety, or chase it into risky unscouted territory for a real information edge over the enemy. Because it keeps moving, vision itself becomes a contested resource instead of a flat, equally-shared one.

Not shown: whether the owl moves on a fixed round timer or a random one, and whether the bonus vision lingers briefly after it leaves or cuts off the instant it relocates - both change how aggressively it's worth chasing.
