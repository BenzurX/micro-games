Mountains level — how do you actually reach the other side?

Redesigned after feedback: the first pass staged four ideas for crossing a solid, impassable mountain wall, but they landed as two disconnected boards - luck of the draw for who spawns with more tiles, with little real interaction. Concept unchanged: mountain tiles are still solid, impassable terrain, splitting the 6x9 board into a top pocket (AI home) and bottom pocket (player home). What changed: all four ideas below make the wall itself a live, contested object - something both players are actively pushing, sieging, digging, or racing toward every round, not a static split decided once at kickoff.

Rough visual sketch only, no game logic wired in. Idea 2 reuses the peak/ranged-siege-arc styling from the previous pass; Idea 4 reuses the pass-tile open/reseal cycle from the previous pass - both are now contestable or temporary instead of a one-time claim.

Design notes - Avalanche Tug

The mechanic: the wall is a contested, moveable resource, not fixed geography. Each round, both players can spend stone to push a shared tug-of-war meter in their favor. Whenever the meter tips past a threshold in either direction, one wall tile crumbles into rubble on the side it tipped toward - that player's pocket physically grows by one tile, into former wall, while the opponent's shrinks. The meter then resets and the fight starts again.

Why this fixes the "luck of the draw" problem: the split between pockets is never decided once at kickoff. It's a running tally both players are actively fighting over every single round, for the entire match - constant, mutual, and directly reactive to how much stone either side is willing to commit, not a fixed geographic accident.

Tradeoff, plainly: stone spent pushing the meter is stone not spent expanding or building elsewhere, so this competes directly with core economy every round. Whoever commits harder generally wins the tug, which risks a snowball if one side falls behind early - worth watching in playtests.

Implementation note: needs a push cost, a rule for whether the meter decays or holds between rounds, and a rule for which tile crumbles first when several remain (fixed order vs. nearest-to-midpoint). Reuses the existing crumble-shake/crumble-dust animation for the actual collapse.

2 — The One Pass

A single, narrow pass tile sits at this chokepoint, unclaimed. Claim it for vision and a discounted siege into the enemy pocket - but the enemy can contest it right back at any time.

You Enemy Solid wall Peak, yours Peak, enemy's
Design notes - The One Pass

The mechanic: a single, scarce pass/peak tile sits at a fixed chokepoint in the wall. Either player can claim it, and holding it grants vision into the opponent's pocket plus a discounted ranged siege into adjacent enemy tiles - no crossing needed. Unlike a one-time claim, the peak can be sieged away and reclaimed by the other player at any time.

Why this fixes the "luck of the draw" problem: because ownership can flip any round, both players must keep spending resources to hold or retake the one tile that matters most on the wall. It's a live, ongoing tug-of-war over a single high-value target, not a static split settled once and forgotten.

Tradeoff, plainly: concentrating all wall interaction into one tile risks a repetitive seesaw fight at the same coordinates every match. The peak needs to be valuable enough to fight over without making every other wall tile feel completely irrelevant by comparison.

Implementation note: needs a contest cost or short cooldown so ownership can't flip every single action, and reuses the peak tile visual and ranged-siege-arc styling already built for the previous Peak Siege concept, with an ownership tint swap added.

Design notes - Tunnel Race

The mechanic: both players dig simultaneously through the wall on two separate, visible progress meters, each advanced by spending stone. Either player can also spend gold to sabotage the opponent's dig, partially resetting their meter. First to fill their meter breaks through and gets to choose where the new permanent crossing opens - an advantage for winning the race.

Why this fixes the "luck of the draw" problem: every point of stone spent digging is directly comparable to the opponent's meter in real time, and every point of gold spent sabotaging is a direct hostile action against their board state. Both players are reacting to the same shared race every round, not building in isolation behind a fixed wall.

Tradeoff, plainly: sabotage can feel purely destructive rather than constructive, and a big early stone lead can snowball - falling behind on the dig invites more sabotage, which discourages the losing side further. Needs tuning so sabotage stays a spicy option rather than a mandatory chore.

Implementation note: needs separate cost tuning for dig vs. sabotage, a rule for who "chooses" the breakthrough tile on a win, and reuses the existing progress-bar styling and crumble animation for the actual breakthrough.

4 — Rockfall Events

You Enemy Solid wall Open, telegraphed Clash
Design notes - Rockfall Events

The mechanic: periodic, telegraphed random events crack open one or two wall tiles for exactly one round before resealing. A visible countdown badge tells both players exactly when and where it will happen.

Why this fixes the "luck of the draw" problem: because the opening is temporary and both players see the identical telegraph on the identical tile at the identical time, both are incentivized to rush the same spot simultaneously. That forces a direct clash on a recurring schedule, rather than two players quietly building economy behind a wall that never asks anything of them.

Tradeoff, plainly: because timing is scheduled rather than resource-driven, a player with a worse economy can still contest evenly - good for parity, but it also means efficient economic play can't buy its way to an advantage around the wall specifically. A missed rush window is pure downside with no partial credit.

Implementation note: needs an event scheduler with some randomized variance in timing (to avoid full predictability), and reuses the pass-tile open/reseal CSS and the crumble/dust animation wholesale from the previous Pass Window concept.
